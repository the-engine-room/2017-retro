2017 The Engine Room Retrospective Supporting social change agents to make the most of data and technology and increase their impact

2017 has been an eventful year, and has inspired all of us at The Engine Room to continue our support work with a growing sense of energy and urgency. From building tools that improve the capacities of non-profit organisations, to advocating for partners and friends on the frontlines of the struggle for human rights, we believe that our work matters now more than ever.

As a support organisation we are always searching for new ways to improve the impact of our partnerships. To that end, , we have adopted new practices and systems that enable our global team and network of partners to do their work well. You can read about some of those in our Support Services and Culture sections below. Through the course of 2017, we continued our work with Matchbox partners and forged new relationships with with new partners around the globe. We researched emerging technology to explore ways it can power the work of social change and shift our society. Individually, we learned new skills, welcomed new family members, explored new territories, and much more. As we jump into 2018, I am proud of the continued growth we’ve each achieved individually and as The Engine Room team. Here’s to a 2017 of support, learning, and social change, and a 2018 of more fruitful partnerships, equitable innovation, and impact!

Alix Dunn

Who We Are

As a support organisation, our work centres on our partners and their needs. We combine cultural and social nuances, expertise on technology and data, and learnings from diverse sectors to tackle real-world challenges.

Over the years we have refined and defined a spectrum of support formats that range from quick and targeted support, to longer-term research and partnerships. This year, we formalised the support we offer to partners, and you can read about them in our What We Do booklet. Each stream of work has its own dynamic workflow, has grown organically and pragmatically, and continues to evolve.

Our Impact

Our work impacts a diverse group of social change actors - from established social change organisations to individuals with powerful ideas; from budding activists, to foundations. We make data and technology more accessible so it can be put to use in making the world a better place. Partners come away with more knowledge and connections, increased confidence in evaluating new data and technology applications, and new ways to do their work. And the social sector grows its capacity to use the tools at its disposal to do even more.

Support Services

Project Accompaniment

aka Build-to-Order (BtO)

Sometimes short, targeted support isn’t enough. For partners who are interested in more in-depth project support, we offer a “Build-to-Order” (BtO) process. In these projects, we work with a partner to shape a strategy, and then manage customised technology design and development projects, research initiatives, and community convenings.

Tech product design and development

Many of the tech tools we built this year were built hand-in-hand with the organisations that wanted to use them:

With K-Monitor and Opora, who are fighting for more transparency and accountability in Hungary and Ukraine, respectively, and other researchers, developers and designers, we co-designed and built a technical solution to address a core challenge: reimagining an open source microtasking platform to pull data out of dense PDFs. We re-used a solution that had been effective for our previous partners Quién Compro, and customised it for K-Monitor and Opora’s contexts. Not only was it fun to build, but it worked well, too.

With SimSim, who support and facilitate citizen participation in public affairs in Morocco, we managed the participatory redesign of their civic engagement platform, Nouabook. Together, we conducted needs assessments, UX research, front and back end design and development, and designed a community engagement strategy.

This year, we also built tools for larger communities of practice:

We kept improving Alidade, an interactive guide to the process of choosing a a tech tool for your social change project, based on extensive community input and feedback. We’re excited about the range of positive feedback about the tool, too!

Research, big and small

Research informs and constitutes much of our work, from introductions to new types of technology, to field-wide reviews. Here’s a look at some of the research outputs we released this year:

With the International Committee of the Red Cross, we dove into the use of messaging apps in humanitarian crises, and saw how they can play a critical role but also raise data privacy and security issues.

We supported Oxfam to better understand how the concept of responsible data is perceived within the confederation, and how their Responsible Data policy is being implemented in practice.

With Making All Voices Count, we reviewed findings from more than 70 research reports and practice papers on design of technology for amplifying citizen voices and encouraging government response. We summarised our findings in a dedicated microsite and audio series.

We worked with the the International Budget Partnership to make technical choices and a plan for migrating their Open Budget survey infrastructure. Read more about their work to measure the level of transparency of national budgets. In the process of this project, we developed a reusable template for researching and choosing between comparable platforms.

Along the way, we hope to shape not just research findings, but how those findings are shared as well! To that end, our team worked with six different partners to introduce Creative Commons licensing into their work.

Bringing people together

We believe in the power of communities, diverse collaboration and mission building, and we aim to bring people together (both virtually and in-person) to tackle challenges, renew connections and build a shared vision for the future.

In Rio de Janeiro — with Ciudadano Inteligente, Ojo Público, TEDIC, FLIP and Fundação Cidadania Inteligente and the support of Open Society Foundations — we brought together 60 advocates, activists, designers, and developers to tackle shared challenges to transparency and accountability in Latin America. The event was full of vibrant ideas and plenty of dancing [ES].

On the heels of that event, we convened a core group to discuss strategies for uniting and using information about Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs). With a mixed group of investigative journalists, activists, data and policy experts from Latin America, we learned how data triggers political influence and political exposure.

For GONG, a Croatian “think-and-do” tank aiming to identify abuses of political power around their country, we facilitated a workshop to help them answer questions and drive decisions about the technology behind their Mosaic of Influence.

Ahead of the Stockholm Internet Forum, and together with Meedan, a social technology company that builds digital tools for global journalism and translation, we convened a group of people to talk about responsible data considerations of using open source information in human rights investigations.

We facilitated exciting discussions about human rights and technology in our community calls, and held a robust discussion with responsible data community practitioners about the impending effects of the GDPR.

What We Learned

Brakes are in cars so that cars can go faster not slower.

For technology and data to be helpful and not harmful, organisations and individuals need to take a considered approach to how it’s deployed. There was an increasing awareness of that in 2017 — we’re excited to see the responsible data community growing — and we anticipate even more growth in the future. By approaching technology and data with safeguards in place, we can use them as tools for even more productive and important work.

Using systems creates more space for creativity - not less.

From further defining how we think about our support work, to adopting new internal policies, 2017 had us exploring new systems to better serve our global team and partners. Along the way, we saw how these structures can be forces for good, not limiting ones: they can make Slack a friendlier and safer space, help us link criteria and chemistry as we pick Matchbox partners, make our hiring and payroll more efficient, and serve as frameworks for making responsible design choices. Our newest processes and systems help us stay organised and flexible in the fast-paced, global contexts we are often in, and we’re all the better for them.

One key to doing support work is shifting power from international organisations to local ones.

The majority of technology support organisations are based in the US or Europe, and are led by individuals with a lot of power, wealth, or privilege. We believe diversity is a competency, so a large part of our work has been to identify, support, and shift equity to local partners, peers and leaders who are often underrepresented. Many of the local organisations we work with don’t have the resources — or access — to certain spheres of influence, so we see part of our role as handing the mic over to them. Sometimes this means referring local organisations for work that we are approached for, or supporting smaller organisations in their grant applications. This might mean they get the funds, not us, and we think this is a good thing. Our goal is to transfer capacity, resources, and power to the people who can make the most of it.

Communication matters — with each other, about our work, to our partners.

Internally, we adopted many intentional practices around communications and ironed out the ways that communication contributes to keeping a remote team moving smoothly. Externally, we saw how the value of our research lies in ensuring that organisations who could use our findings have it at their fingertips, when they need it (and in diverse formats like audio series, too). We prioritised engagement and communications (hiring a full-time Communications Coordinator) and will be working more on this in 2018.

Building A Healthy Culture

We are a team of 16 core members based in 10 countries on four continents. We grew by 5 new people this year, welcoming Nicole Anand, Matt Foster, Laura Guzman, Nonso Jideofor, and Elissa Williams. We said farewell to two team members, Tin Geber and Roz Zavras.

All of the work we do is based on the strong foundation our operations team lays.

In 2017, we wound down our Norwegian operations, and are now solely operating as a US 501c3. We adopted new HR platforms and practices, too; this might not sound thrilling, but it keeps our team members and consultants working smoothly! Part of this is the release of our Candidate Roster form, where applicants interested in working with us can let us know more about their background and interests.

Maintaining a team culture that is kind, supportive, productive and diverse doesn’t happen out of the blue.

This year, our Operations team poured time into policies, events (e.g. our team retreat), and guidelines (e.g. how to give feedback) that centered the well-being of our staff. This made it possible for team members to grow into new roles as they followed their skills and interests. This flexibility also made it possible for our Executive Director, Alix, to take a sabbatical and pursue a fellowship with Open Society Foundations.
